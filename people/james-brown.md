# James Brown

**Category**: People
**Key figures**: James Brown (musician, 1933–2006), Bobby Byrd (collaborator and co-founder), Maceo Parker (saxophonist and longtime band member), Clyde Stubblefield (drummer), Alfred "Pee Wee" Ellis (musical director)

## Summary

James Brown, universally known as "The Godfather of Soul" and "The Hardest Working Man in Show Business," died on December 25, 2006, in Atlanta, Georgia, at age 73 from congestive heart failure exacerbated by pneumonia. He had been admitted to Emory Crawford Long Hospital on December 24 after his personal physician found him in severe respiratory distress at his Atlanta home. His death marked the end of a performing career spanning more than five decades and the life of one of the most consequential figures in 20th-century popular music.

Brown's influence extended across funk, soul, rhythm and blues, hip-hop, and virtually every form of contemporary popular music that followed him. He holds the distinction of being the most sampled recording artist in music history: more than 10,000 documented samples in commercially released recordings draw from his catalog, with his drum breaks—particularly Clyde Stubblefield's pattern on "Funky Drummer" (1970)—forming the rhythmic foundation of hip-hop production from the mid-1980s through the present. Rolling Stone ranked him seventh on its list of the 100 Greatest Artists of All Time; Joel Whitburn's analysis of *Billboard* R&B chart history from 1942 to 2010 placed him first among all R&B artists across that span.

## Early Life and Career Origins

### Background

James Joseph Brown was born on May 3, 1933, in Barnwell, South Carolina—though some sources list the nearby community of Beech Island—and grew up in Augusta, Georgia, in conditions of extreme poverty. Largely self-taught, he absorbed gospel from churches, blues from Augusta's radio and jukeboxes, and rhythm patterns from street-corner performers. He was arrested for petty theft at age 16 and sentenced to 8–16 years in the Georgia Juvenile Training Institute; he served approximately three years before his early release in 1952, facilitated in part by the advocacy of Bobby Byrd, a gospel singer who had visited the facility. Byrd became Brown's most important early collaborator and a founding member of his group.

### The Famous Flames

Released into Byrd's care, Brown joined his musical group, which reorganized as The Famous Flames. The group signed with Federal Records (a King Records subsidiary) in 1956, releasing "Please, Please, Please"—a song Brown had first performed at a talent contest—on March 3, 1956. The single reached number five on the *Billboard* R&B chart, establishing Brown's commercial viability. Over the following three years, the group toured relentlessly across the South and Midwest, playing what industry observers called the "chitlin' circuit"—the network of Black-owned and Black-friendly venues that served African American audiences during the era of racial segregation.

"Try Me" (1958), released on Federal, reached number one on the R&B chart and crossed into the pop top forty—Brown's first pop chart success. The song's commercial performance gave King Records confidence to invest in the group, leading to a fully staffed touring band by 1959–1960 that would evolve into the JBs.

## Musical Innovations

### The Birth of Funk

Brown's most consequential musical innovation was the systematic development of funk as a distinct genre, a process that unfolded between approximately 1964 and 1970. The transition is typically traced through several recordings:

- **"Out of Sight" (1964)**: An early move toward rhythmic emphasis on the first beat ("the one") rather than the second and fourth beats ("the backbeat") typical of R&B.
- **"Papa's Got a Brand New Bag" (1965)**: The breakthrough funk single, characterized by percussive guitar, the "on the one" rhythmic emphasis, and a shift of harmonic interest from chord changes to groove. It reached number one on the R&B chart and number eight on the pop chart; Brown won his first Grammy Award (Best R&B Recording) for this song in 1966.
- **"Cold Sweat" (1967)**: Co-written with Maceo Parker and Alfred "Pee Wee" Ellis, this recording introduced the concept of a full groove constructed almost entirely from rhythmic interplay among bass, drums, and horn accents, with melody reduced to brief melodic fragments. Many musicologists identify "Cold Sweat" as the first fully realized funk recording.
- **"Get Up (I Feel Like Being a) Sex Machine" (1970)**: A definitive funk statement, built over drummer John "Jabo" Starks's groove and Bootsy Collins's bass line, recorded with the young band that would become the J.B.'s.

Brown's central rhythmic concept—placing the emphasis on beat one and treating every instrument as a percussive element—was a fundamental departure from the gospel-influenced rhythmic structure of soul and R&B and directly prefigured the loop-based production that became the foundation of hip-hop.

### Vocal Style and Stagecraft

Brown's vocals defied standard pop categorization: he built performances from grunts, screams, spoken asides, and hollers as much as from sung melody, extending African American vocal traditions from gospel and field hollers into a secular performance context. His stage act—which included cape routines, splits, and exhausting physical performances averaging over two hours—was legendary among performers and audiences alike. He routinely performed 330+ shows per year through the late 1960s, a pace that earned the "Hardest Working Man" designation.

## Civil Rights Era and Social Role

### The Boston Concert (April 5, 1968)

The evening following the assassination of Martin Luther King Jr. on April 4, 1968, Brown was scheduled to perform a concert at the Boston Garden. City officials, fearing the kind of unrest that had already broken out in dozens of American cities, debated cancelling the show. Boston Mayor Kevin White—on the advice of city officials and at Brown's own insistence—decided instead to broadcast the concert live on WGBH television and allow the Garden to open. Approximately 2,000 people attended in person; tens of thousands watched on television. Brown repeatedly urged the audience to remain calm and peaceful, pausing the performance when small disturbances broke out on the floor. Boston experienced minimal unrest compared to peer cities; both Brown and Mayor White later cited the concert as a critical factor. The event is documented in the 2017 documentary *The Night James Brown Saved Boston*.

### "Say It Loud – I'm Black and I'm Proud" (1968)

Recorded in August 1968 and released on October 22, 1968, this single functioned as both a funk record and an explicit declaration of Black identity and political self-determination, recorded with the participation of approximately 30 children whose voices appear on the chorus. It reached number one on the R&B chart and number ten on the pop chart, making it one of the most commercially successful explicitly political records in American history to that point. Brown later said the song was a direct response to King's assassination and the urgency of the post-1968 political moment for Black Americans.

Brown's relationship with mainstream political structures was complex: he endorsed President Richard Nixon in 1972, a decision that cost him significant credibility among civil rights activists but that Brown defended as a pragmatic effort to direct federal attention to Black economic development. He also endorsed President Jimmy Carter in 1976, reflecting a political independence from party allegiances that paralleled his musical independence from genre conventions.

## Career Highlights and Later Years

### Recording and Touring

Brown's catalog across the 1960s and 1970s generated 17 number-one R&B hits and numerous pop chart successes. His most commercially productive period was 1965–1975, during which he maintained a grueling schedule of recording (sometimes producing multiple albums per year) and touring. By 1968 he was among the highest-paid performers in the United States, grossing an estimated $4.5 million annually from touring—a figure without parallel among Black entertainers of that era.

He continued recording and performing through the 1980s and 1990s, adapting to the disco and funk crossover of the 1970s and achieving renewed mainstream visibility when "Living in America" (1985), recorded for the *Rocky IV* soundtrack, returned him to the pop top ten and won the Grammy Award for Best R&B Vocal Performance, Male, at the 28th Grammy Awards (1986).

The Rock and Roll Hall of Fame inducted Brown in its inaugural class of ten performers on January 23, 1986, alongside Chuck Berry, Ray Charles, Sam Cooke, Fats Domino, the Everly Brothers, Buddy Holly, Jerry Lee Lewis, Little Richard, and Elvis Presley.

### Legal Issues and Personal Difficulties

Brown's later career was marked by significant personal and legal difficulties. In 1988 he fled from South Carolina police in a car chase that ended in his arrest on charges including assault with intent to kill and drug possession; he was convicted and sentenced to six years in prison, of which he served approximately two years before his release in February 1991. He received a Grammy Lifetime Achievement Award in 1992. He continued to record and tour through the 1990s and into the 2000s, performing hundreds of shows per year into his seventies.

## Death and Funeral

Brown's declining health in late 2006 was not publicly known; he had continued performing and granting interviews in the months before his death. On December 23, 2006, his personal dentist, Dr. Marvin Crawford, visited Brown at home in Augusta and found him seriously ill; he was transported to Emory Crawford Long Hospital in Atlanta, where he died at approximately 1:45 a.m. on December 25, 2006.

Public viewing of Brown's body took place at the Apollo Theater in Harlem, New York, on December 28, 2006—a venue inextricably associated with his early career through his legendary 1962 live album *Live at the Apollo*, recorded there without the label's initial support and eventually selling over a million copies. His casket arrived in a gold horse-drawn carriage outside the Apollo before thousands of mourners. A second public viewing was held in Augusta, Georgia.

The funeral service was held at the James Brown Arena in Augusta (a venue named after him in 1999) on December 30, 2006, attended by approximately 9,000 people including Al Sharpton, Michael Jackson, and numerous musicians whose careers Brown's work had directly influenced. Brown was initially interred at his estate in Beech Island, South Carolina, though disputes over his estate delayed the arrangement of his final resting place.

## Legacy

### Sampling and Hip-Hop

Brown's rhythmic recordings—particularly the drum breaks of Clyde Stubblefield and John "Jabo" Starks—became the raw material of hip-hop when producers in the early 1980s began isolating and looping recorded rhythmic passages. "Funky Drummer" (1970), featuring Stubblefield, alone has been sampled in over 1,000 commercially released recordings, including tracks by N.W.A, Public Enemy, Ice Cube, Sinéad O'Connor, and Janet Jackson. Brown's influence on hip-hop is thus not merely direct (through his own sampled recordings) but structural: the loop-based, rhythm-first sonic vocabulary he established became foundational to the genre, and his catalog remained a primary source for producers refining it.

### Musical Rankings and Recognition

- Rolling Stone ranked him **seventh** on the 100 Greatest Artists of All Time (2004 list).
- Joel Whitburn ranked him **first** among R&B artists from 1942 to 2010 in cumulative Billboard R&B chart performance.
- He holds at least **17 number-one R&B singles**, more than any artist in that era.
- He was awarded the **Grammy Lifetime Achievement Award** in 1992.
- The James Brown Arena in Augusta, Georgia, was named in his honor in 1999.
- BET named him the **greatest entertainer of all time** in a 2017 special.

## Significance

James Brown's death on December 25, 2006, marked the end of an era for American popular music. He stands as the central progenitor of funk as a distinct genre, the originator of a rhythmic vocabulary that underlies contemporary hip-hop production, and one of the most consequential live performers in the history of popular entertainment. His civil rights-era activism—particularly the April 5, 1968, Boston concert—established him as a cultural leader whose impact extended beyond entertainment into civic life. His influence continues to shape popular music production in the 21st century in ways that make him, by many measures, the most sampled and formally influential recording artist of the 20th century.
