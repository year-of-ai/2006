# Muhammad Yunus and Grameen Bank Win the Nobel Peace Prize

**Category**: Society & Economics
**Key figures**: Muhammad Yunus (economist and founder of Grameen Bank); Grameen Bank (Bangladeshi microfinance institution); the Norwegian Nobel Committee

## Summary

On **October 13, 2006**, the Norwegian Nobel Committee announced that the **Nobel Peace Prize for 2006** would be awarded, in two equal parts, to Bangladeshi economist **Muhammad Yunus** and the **Grameen Bank** he founded, "for their efforts to create economic and social development from below." The committee argued that lasting peace could not be achieved unless large population groups found ways to break out of poverty, and it credited microcredit with being one such means. Yunus became the first Bangladeshi to receive a Nobel Prize.

Yunus had pioneered the modern practice of **microcredit** — extending very small, collateral-free loans to impoverished people, especially in rural Bangladesh, who were excluded from the conventional banking system. Beginning with a modest experiment lending small sums to villagers in the 1970s, he formally established Grameen Bank in 1983 on the principle that access to credit is a fundamental means of fighting poverty. By the time of the award, the bank had lent to **more than seven million borrowers**, with over **95 percent** of its loans going to women, and it operated on a group-lending model in which borrowers supported one another's repayment. The bank reported repayment rates far higher than skeptics had predicted for loans to the very poor.

The Nobel Committee's decision brought global recognition to microfinance as a development tool and to the broader idea that market-based mechanisms could be harnessed to empower the world's poorest people. The prize was formally presented at the award ceremony in Oslo on December 10, 2006.

## Significance

The 2006 Peace Prize marked the international mainstreaming of **microfinance**. Grameen Bank became a template replicated by microcredit institutions in more than one hundred countries, and the award lent powerful legitimacy to the argument that poverty alleviation and financial inclusion were themselves foundations of peace and stability. Coming from a committee more often associated with statesmen, peace negotiators, and human-rights campaigners, the choice signaled a broadened understanding of what constitutes peace-building — encompassing economic empowerment "from below" rather than diplomacy alone.

The prize also elevated the role of **women's economic empowerment** in development thinking, given Grameen's overwhelmingly female borrower base and the evidence that lending to women produced strong social returns for families and communities. In the years that followed, microfinance drew increased scrutiny — over interest rates, over-indebtedness, and questions about how much it actually reduced poverty — but the 2006 award remains a landmark moment that placed grassroots economic development firmly on the global agenda and made Yunus one of the most recognized figures in international development.
