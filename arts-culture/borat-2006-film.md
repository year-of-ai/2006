# Borat: Cultural Learnings of America for Make Benefit Glorious Nation of Kazakhstan

**Category**: Arts & Culture  
**Key figures**: Sacha Baron Cohen (writer, star), Larry Charles (director), Jay Roach (producer), Anthony Hines, Peter Baynham, Dan Mazer (co-writers), Ken Davitian (Azamat), 20th Century Fox

## Summary

"Borat: Cultural Learnings of America for Make Benefit Glorious Nation of Kazakhstan" premiered at the Toronto International Film Festival in September 2006, opened in limited release on November 3, 2006, and became one of 2006's most talked-about films — a provocative mockumentary comedy that blended scripted scenes with hidden-camera ambush humor. Created by and starring British comedian Sacha Baron Cohen as the eponymous Kazakh news reporter, the film follows Borat Sagdiyev's satirical journey across the United States, ostensibly to make a documentary but actually engaging in often offensive, profane, and shocking interactions with unsuspecting Americans.

The film's premise was deceptively simple: place an outrageous foreign character with absurd beliefs (anti-Semitic, misogynistic, crude) into real American situations and film the reactions. Baron Cohen's character embodied grotesque stereotypes and used absurdist humor to expose prejudice, ignorance, and social hypocrisy. The film was structured as a mockumentary with a thin narrative arc — Borat traveling from New York to California to meet Pamela Anderson — but the core value lay in its hidden-camera sequences capturing authentic American responses to Borat's provocations.

## Production

Principal photography took place in 2005–2006, with U.S. segments shot guerrilla-style across multiple states. Romanian villages (primarily Glod, in Dâmbovița County) stood in for Kazakhstan, a casting choice that later prompted protests from Romanian villagers who claimed they were deceived about the nature of the footage. The "Kazakhstan" government building and rural scenes were Romanian locations reframed through editing.

The screenplay, co-written by Baron Cohen, Anthony Hines, Peter Baynham, and Dan Mazer — all veterans of his *Da Ali G Show* — was partly structured and partly improvised around Baron Cohen's character work. Director Larry Charles, known for his *Seinfeld* and *Curb Your Enthusiasm* writing credits, worked with a skeleton crew to remain undetected during ambush sequences. The total production budget was approximately $18 million, funded by 20th Century Fox after the success of the *Ali G* character on HBO.

## Box Office and Critical Reception

Released by 20th Century Fox, *Borat* opened at #1 with $26.5 million domestically in its wide-release weekend — an extraordinary number for a film with such transgressive content. It earned $128.5 million in North America and approximately $133 million internationally, for a worldwide gross exceeding $261 million against its $18 million budget — a return of roughly 14:1.

Critical reception was overwhelmingly positive among mainstream reviewers. The film holds a 91% score on Rotten Tomatoes and a Metacritic score of 89 out of 100. Roger Ebert gave it four stars, describing it as a "comic stunner." At the 79th Academy Awards (February 2007), the screenplay earned an Oscar nomination for Best Adapted Screenplay — an unusual distinction for a comedy nominally based on a television character.

## Cultural Reaction and Controversies

The film's release sparked immediate and multilayered controversy:

**Kazakhstan's diplomatic response**: The Kazakhstan government initially threatened legal action and placed full-page ads in U.S. newspapers asserting the country's modernity and progress. President Nursultan Nazarbayev complained to President George W. Bush about the film during a September 2006 White House visit. Paradoxically, Kazakhstan's tourism board later reported a significant increase in tourist inquiries following the film's release, with some estimates suggesting a 300% spike in web searches about Kazakhstan.

**Lawsuits from participants**: Several individuals who appeared in the film without full informed consent filed suit. The most publicized case involved a group of University of South Carolina fraternity members who appeared in a scene drunkenly expressing racist sentiments — they sued alleging misrepresentation, but the case was eventually dismissed. Romanian villagers from Glod filed a lawsuit in 2008, claiming they were misled about the project.

**Ethical debate**: Academics and media critics immediately debated whether the film's satirical intent justified its methods. Feminist scholars criticized the film's extensive use of misogyny even in the service of critique. Jewish-American organizations were divided — the Anti-Defamation League issued a nuanced statement acknowledging Baron Cohen's Jewish identity and satirical intent while noting the film's anti-Semitic content could reinforce rather than subvert stereotypes.

## Political Satire Context

The film's U.S. release in November 2006 coincided with the midterm elections that handed Democrats control of both chambers of Congress — a referendum partly on the Iraq War and the Bush administration's policies. The *Borat* film, with its satirical portrait of American provincialism, nativism, and uncritical patriotism, resonated with audiences amid this political climate. Several critics noted that the film's most uncomfortable scenes — a rodeo crowd cheering Borat's off-color anti-terror "jokes," fraternizing with segregationist sentiments — felt particularly pointed in the charged atmosphere of 2006 American politics. ## Significance

*Borat* represents a pivotal moment in 21st-century comedy and media ethics. It codified the "ambush mockumentary" as a mainstream film genre, spawning imitators and a 2020 sequel (*Borat Subsequent Moviefilm*, filmed during COVID-19). The film's success highlighted the mid-2000s appetite for provocative, boundary-pushing entertainment that challenged social norms and political correctness — a cultural tension that would intensify through the 2010s.

More fundamentally, *Borat* raised unresolved questions about comedic consent and exploitation that remain live issues in the 2020s: To what extent can comedy justify hidden-camera ambush and humiliation of ordinary people? When does satirical intent dissolve into bullying? The film's producers settled numerous lawsuits from people featured without permission, acknowledging the ethical tensions Baron Cohen himself has since grappled with in interviews, including a 2021 discussion about whether the 2006 film could have been made in the social-media era.

*Borat* also marked Sacha Baron Cohen's breakthrough into mainstream cinema (following his HBO/British TV cult fame from *Da Ali G Show*, 2000–2004). Its success propelled him into a career of provocative characters and films — Bruno (2009), *The Dictator* (2012), *The Trial of the Chicago 7* (2020) — making him one of the defining satirists of the 2000s–2010s.

The film is a cultural artifact of 2006: it captured the irreverent, transgressive-comedy zeitgeist of the mid-2000s before social media, cancel culture, and heightened awareness of consent would reshape what could be laughed at in public. That same year, [An Inconvenient Truth](../arts-culture/an-inconvenient-truth.md) and [Casino Royale](../arts-culture/casino-royale-daniel-craig.md) also defined the cinematic landscape of 2006.
