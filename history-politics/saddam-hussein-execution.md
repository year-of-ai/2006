# Execution of Saddam Hussein

**Category**: History & Politics
**Key figures**: Saddam Hussein (former President of Iraq); Nouri al-Maliki (Prime Minister of Iraq); Rauf Rashid Abd al-Rahman (Chief Judge, Iraqi High Tribunal); Barzan Ibrahim al-Tikriti (co-defendant, Saddam's half-brother); Awad Hamed al-Bandar (co-defendant, former Chief Justice)

## Summary

Saddam Hussein, the former President of Iraq, was executed by hanging on **December 30, 2006**, at approximately 6:00 a.m. local time at "Camp Justice" (Muthanna Airfield), a joint Iraqi-U.S. military base in the Kadhimiya district of northeastern Baghdad. He was confirmed dead a few minutes after the trap door fell, and the Iraqi government released an official statement confirming the execution within hours. Saddam, who had been captured hiding in a "spider hole" near Ad-Dawr by U.S. troops on December 13, 2003, had ruled Iraq from July 16, 1979 until he was deposed during the U.S.-led invasion of March–April 2003.

The execution followed his conviction by the **Iraqi High Tribunal** (IHT) for crimes against humanity in connection with the **Dujail massacre**—the killing of 148 Shia residents of the town of Dujail in 1982, carried out in retaliation for a failed assassination attempt on July 8, 1982. The trial opened October 19, 2005, and on November 5, 2006, the tribunal sentenced Saddam to death by hanging. On December 26, 2006, the Iraqi Court of Cassation upheld the sentence and ordered it carried out within 30 days. Co-defendants Barzan Ibrahim al-Tikriti and Awad Hamed al-Bandar were executed on January 15, 2007.

The execution fell on the first day of **Eid al-Adha**, a major Islamic holiday, drawing criticism for its timing. An unauthorized mobile phone video, leaked shortly afterward, showed witnesses taunting Saddam with sectarian chants at the gallows—footage that circulated globally and drew sharp international rebukes.

## Background

### Saddam Hussein's Rule (1979–2003)

Born April 28, 1937, in Al-Awja near Tikrit, Saddam Hussein rose through the Ba'ath Party and became President of Iraq on July 16, 1979 after forcing the ailing Ahmed Hassan al-Bakr from power. His three-and-a-half decade career in Iraqi politics was marked by extreme violence used to consolidate and maintain power. Among the most serious crimes attributed to his government:

- **Iran-Iraq War (1980–1988)**: Iraq invaded Iran on September 22, 1980. Approximately 500,000 soldiers died on both sides. Iraqi forces used chemical weapons—mustard gas and nerve agents—against Iranian troops, with documented instances in 1983–1988.
- **Anfal campaign (1986–1989)**: A systematic military campaign against Kurdish populations in northern Iraq, involving mass killings estimated to have caused 50,000 to 182,000 deaths. The **Halabja chemical attack** of March 16–17, 1988 killed an estimated 3,200–5,000 civilians.
- **Invasion of Kuwait (August 2, 1990)** and the subsequent Gulf War (1991), in which a U.S.-led coalition expelled Iraqi forces.
- **Suppression of the 1991 Shia and Kurdish uprisings**: Following the Gulf War ceasefire, Saddam crushed uprisings in the south and north; tens of thousands of Shia were killed and hundreds of thousands displaced.

### The Dujail Massacre (1982)

On **July 8, 1982**, gunmen from the Islamic Dawa Party ambushed Saddam Hussein's motorcade in the Shia town of Dujail, northeast of Baghdad. The assassination attempt failed. In retaliation, the Republican Guard and intelligence services descended on Dujail: at least **148 men and boys were executed** after mock trials, approximately 1,500 residents (including women and children) were arrested and detained in desert camps, the town's date palm groves—the economic lifeblood of the community—were bulldozed, and hundreds of homes were demolished. The town remained largely depopulated and under surveillance for years. Documents seized after 2003 confirmed Saddam personally signed the execution orders.

## The Trial

### Iraqi High Tribunal

The **Iraqi Special Tribunal** was established by the Coalition Provisional Authority in December 2003 (Law No. 10 of the Governing Council) and was renamed the **Iraqi High Tribunal** in 2005. It was mandated to try senior Ba'ath Party and government officials for genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes committed between July 17, 1968 and May 1, 2003.

Saddam was charged alongside seven co-defendants for the Dujail massacre. The trial was presided over by Chief Judge **Rizgar Mohammed Amin** from its opening on **October 19, 2005**, but he resigned in January 2006 citing governmental pressure. His replacement, **Rauf Rashid Abd al-Rahman**, led the proceedings to verdict. The trial was frequently disrupted: defense lawyers were assassinated (two were killed during proceedings), Saddam and other defendants boycotted hearings, and the defense challenged the court's legitimacy as a creation of the occupying power.

### Verdict and Sentencing

On **November 5, 2006**, the IHT found Saddam guilty of crimes against humanity for the Dujail massacre and sentenced him to death by hanging. Co-defendants Barzan Ibrahim al-Tikriti and Awad Hamed al-Bandar received the same sentence; four others received sentences ranging from 15 years to life.

Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, and the International Bar Association criticized the trial for procedural shortcomings including the assassinations of defence lawyers, constraints on the defence's ability to cross-examine witnesses, and the early removal of Amin. Iraq was concurrently preparing a second, larger trial for the Anfal campaign, which was underway when Saddam was executed.

## The Execution

### Final Hours

Following the December 26, 2006 appellate ruling, the Iraqi government moved quickly. Saddam was transferred from U.S. military custody to Iraqi custody in the early hours of December 30. Iraqi government officials present included national security advisor Mowaffak al-Rubaie and other senior officials. Witnesses also included representatives from the Dujail community.

At approximately **6:00 a.m.**, Saddam was led to the gallows. He reportedly appeared composed and declined a hood. As the noose was placed, witnesses—including guards—began taunting him with chants of "**Muqtada! Muqtada! Muqtada!**" in reference to Shia cleric Muqtada al-Sadr, whose father had been killed under Saddam's orders. Saddam responded defiantly before the trap door opened. He died instantly from a broken neck.

### The Leaked Video

An official recording was made. In addition, a guard filmed the events on a mobile phone; that footage—showing the taunting and the moments leading to the drop—was leaked to Arab satellite networks and then to YouTube within hours of the execution. The video's worldwide circulation prompted international criticism and deepened sectarian tensions inside Iraq. Prime Minister Nouri al-Maliki's office arrested a guard suspected of filming it. U.S. President George W. Bush stated he wished "the proceedings had gone in a more dignified way."

The timing on the first morning of **Eid al-Adha** was also criticized, including by Sunni Arab governments, as the holiday is considered a sacred and joyful occasion; many Sunni Iraqis and regional observers viewed the execution's timing as a deliberate sectarian provocation.

## International and Domestic Reaction

**United States**: President Bush called the execution "an important milestone on Iraq's course to becoming a democracy." The Bush administration had expressed frustration with the speed of the Iraqi government's move to execute Saddam before the Anfal trial concluded, but did not intervene.

**European Union and the United Nations**: The EU reiterated its opposition to capital punishment and called for the sentence to be commuted; UN Secretary-General Kofi Annan expressed regret. UK Foreign Secretary Margaret Beckett said the UK "respect[ed] Iraq's democratic decision" but noted British opposition to the death penalty.

**Arab world**: Reactions split largely along sectarian lines. Many Shia Iraqis and Lebanese celebrated. In Sunni-majority countries and communities—including Jordan, the Palestinian territories, and Sunni regions of Iraq—there were protests and expressions of condemnation.

**Iraq**: A dusk-to-dawn curfew was imposed in Baghdad on December 30. Despite it, sporadic violence occurred. The sectarian civil war that had escalated through 2006 continued unabated; the execution did not stabilise the security situation.

## Significance

The execution closed the chapter of Saddam Hussein's direct political and physical presence in Iraq, ending the life of one of the twentieth century's most brutal authoritarian rulers and a man whose decisions had cost hundreds of thousands of Iraqi and Iranian lives. It was one of the few instances in the modern era in which a former head of state was tried by a domestic successor tribunal and executed, and it took place under the shadow of a continuing foreign military occupation.

Coming amid the worst phase of Iraq's post-invasion sectarian violence—an estimated 34,452 Iraqi civilian deaths were recorded in 2006 alone—the execution was received very differently across the country: welcomed by many Shia and Kurdish Iraqis who had suffered under his rule; condemned by many Sunnis who viewed the rushed process and taunting as victor's justice rather than impartial law. The leaked video in particular became a lasting symbol of the execution's chaotic and divisive nature.

The broader trial of Saddam Hussein established a precedent for post-conflict accountability that would be examined in relation to subsequent international justice processes, though its procedural shortcomings were equally cited as cautionary examples by international law scholars.
