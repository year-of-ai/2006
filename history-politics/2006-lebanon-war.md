# 2006 Lebanon War

**Category**: History & Politics
**Key figures**: Ehud Olmert (Israeli Prime Minister); Hassan Nasrallah (Hezbollah Secretary-General); Fouad Siniora (Lebanese Prime Minister); Amir Peretz (Israeli Defense Minister); Dan Halutz (IDF Chief of Staff); United Nations Security Council; UNIFIL

## Summary

The **2006 Lebanon War** — also called the **Israel–Hezbollah War** or, in Lebanon, the **July War** — was a 34-day armed conflict fought between **Israel** and the Lebanese militant group **Hezbollah** from **12 July to 14 August 2006**. It was the most serious confrontation between the two since Israel's withdrawal from southern Lebanon in 2000.

The war began on **12 July 2006**, when Hezbollah fighters launched a cross-border raid (codenamed "Operation Truthful Promise"), ambushing an Israeli patrol near Zar'it: three Israeli soldiers were killed and two — Ehud Goldwasser and Eldad Regev — were captured, with five more soldiers killed in a failed rescue attempt. Israel responded with a large-scale air campaign, a naval and air **blockade** of Lebanon, and a ground incursion into the south, while Hezbollah fired thousands of rockets into northern Israel.

A ceasefire under **United Nations Security Council Resolution 1701**, adopted unanimously on **11 August 2006**, took effect on the morning of **14 August 2006**. The naval blockade was lifted on **8 September 2006**. The fighting killed roughly **1,100–1,200 Lebanese** (the large majority civilians) and about **165 Israelis** (around 120 soldiers and over 40 civilians), and displaced an estimated **one million Lebanese** and several hundred thousand Israelis. Both Israel and Hezbollah claimed victory; the war is widely regarded as militarily inconclusive.

## The Outbreak and the Combatants

The immediate trigger was Hezbollah's 12 July cross-border operation, which the group framed as an effort to capture Israeli soldiers to exchange for Lebanese and Palestinian prisoners. Israel's government, led by Prime Minister **Ehud Olmert**, treated the raid as an act of war emanating from Lebanese territory and held the Lebanese state responsible for Hezbollah's actions.

The conflict pitted the **Israel Defense Forces (IDF)** — a conventional state military with air superiority — against **Hezbollah**, a non-state armed movement and political party operating from fortified positions in southern Lebanon, supported by allied factions including the Amal Movement. The asymmetry between a state army and an entrenched guerrilla force became one of the war's defining features and a widely studied case in modern military doctrine.

## The Military Campaign

**Israeli operations.** Over 34 days the Israeli Air Force flew nearly **12,000 combat missions**, and Israeli forces fired roughly **170,000 artillery shells** and thousands of naval rounds. Airstrikes destroyed the runways of **Beirut–Rafic Hariri International Airport**, struck bridges, roads, fuel depots, and the southern Beirut district of Dahieh (a Hezbollah stronghold), and imposed an air-and-sea blockade. In the final days of the war the IDF mounted an expanded ground offensive into southern Lebanon up toward the Litani River.

**Hezbollah's response.** Hezbollah fired an estimated **3,970–4,228 rockets** into northern Israel over the course of the war — at times exceeding 100 per day — the overwhelming majority being short-range 122 mm Katyusha rockets that reached cities including Haifa. Hezbollah fighters engaged the IDF in sustained guerrilla combat from prepared bunkers and used anti-tank guided missiles to damage Israeli armor, inflicting unexpectedly heavy losses on advancing ground units.

## Casualties and Displacement

Casualty estimates vary by source but cluster within a consistent range:

- **Lebanese**: approximately **1,100–1,300 killed**, the large majority civilians; Human Rights Watch and Hezbollah accounts put Hezbollah combatant deaths at roughly **250**, alongside dozens of Lebanese Armed Forces and security personnel.
- **Israeli**: about **165 killed** in total — roughly **120 IDF soldiers** and **more than 40 civilians**, the latter largely from rocket fire.
- **Displacement**: an estimated **one million Lebanese** were forced to flee, while several hundred thousand Israelis left or were evacuated from northern communities within rocket range.

The war also caused extensive damage to Lebanese civilian infrastructure and a major coastal oil spill after the bombing of the Jiyeh power station, with significant long-term reconstruction costs.

## The Ceasefire: UN Security Council Resolution 1701

International diplomacy culminated in **UN Security Council Resolution 1701**, adopted **unanimously on 11 August 2006**. The resolution called for a "full cessation of hostilities," the withdrawal of Israeli forces from southern Lebanon in parallel with the deployment of the **Lebanese Armed Forces** and an enlarged **United Nations Interim Force in Lebanon (UNIFIL)**, the disarmament of armed groups so that no authority other than the Lebanese state would hold weapons, and the establishment of an area between the Blue Line and the Litani River free of armed personnel other than the Lebanese army and UNIFIL.

The ceasefire took effect at **8:00 a.m. local time on 14 August 2006**. The Lebanese army began deploying into the south on **17 August**, and UNIFIL was substantially expanded. Key provisions — particularly the disarmament of Hezbollah and the full clearing of the border zone — were never implemented, and Hezbollah retained its arsenal.

## Significance

The 2006 Lebanon War carried consequences well beyond its 34 days:

1. **A studied case of asymmetric warfare.** Hezbollah's ability to absorb a sustained air campaign, keep firing rockets until the final day, and bloody Israeli ground forces reshaped military thinking about conflicts between conventional armies and entrenched non-state actors.
2. **Domestic political fallout in Israel.** The perceived failure to achieve clear objectives prompted the official **Winograd Commission** inquiry, which judged the war "a missed opportunity" and faulted Israel's political and military leadership, contributing to the later resignations of senior officials.
3. **Hezbollah's regional standing.** By withstanding the IDF and claiming victory, Hezbollah and its leader **Hassan Nasrallah** gained prestige across parts of the Arab world, while consolidating the group's domestic political and military power in Lebanon.
4. **A lasting but fragile framework.** Resolution 1701 and the enlarged UNIFIL mandate ended the fighting and shaped the Israel–Lebanon border for years afterward, even as its core disarmament terms went unfulfilled, leaving the underlying conflict unresolved.

The war was also one of the geopolitical risk events that rattled global markets in mid-2006: its outbreak on 12 July coincided with the week that [crude oil prices reached then-record highs](../society-economics/2006-crude-oil-prices.md), as traders priced in fears that a wider Middle East confrontation could disrupt regional energy supplies.
