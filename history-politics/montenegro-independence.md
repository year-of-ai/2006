# Montenegro Declares Independence

**Category:** History & Politics  
**Key figures:** Milo Đukanović (Prime Minister of Montenegro), Svetozar Marović (President of Serbia and Montenegro), Vojislav Koštunica (Prime Minister of Serbia), Javier Solana (EU High Representative)

## Summary

On June 3, 2006, Montenegro formally declared independence, dissolving the State Union of Serbia and Montenegro and establishing itself as Europe's newest sovereign state. The declaration followed a May 21 referendum in which 55.5% of Montenegrin voters approved independence, narrowly exceeding the required 55% threshold set by the European Union as a condition for recognising the result. Montenegro's path to independence was constitutionally mandated under the Constitutional Charter of Serbia and Montenegro (adopted February 4, 2003), which established a three-year waiting period before either republic could hold a referendum. After the state Referendum Commission confirmed the official result on May 31, the Montenegrin parliament adopted a formal declaration of independence on June 3, and Serbia acknowledged the dissolution on June 5.

## Background

### The Dissolution of Yugoslavia

Montenegro's quest for independence was rooted in the violent disintegration of the Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia between 1991 and 1995. As Slovenia, Croatia, Bosnia-Herzegovina, and North Macedonia declared independence in 1991–1992, Montenegro initially chose to remain in a rump federation with Serbia, forming the Federal Republic of Yugoslavia (FRY) on April 27, 1992. The FRY became internationally isolated following its role in the Bosnian War and the Kosovo conflict (1998–1999), during which NATO air strikes targeted Belgrade and Montenegrin infrastructure.

By the late 1990s, Montenegrin Prime Minister Milo Đukanović had pivoted sharply away from alignment with Slobodan Milošević's Serbia and toward Western integration. After the fall of Milošević in October 2000, the two republics restructured their relationship. Under pressure from the EU—which feared a destabilising chain of Balkan break-ups—Montenegro and Serbia agreed in 2002 to form a looser arrangement rather than proceed immediately to independence. The resulting Constitutional Charter of February 4, 2003, created the **State Union of Serbia and Montenegro**, replacing the FRY. The charter permitted either republic to call an independence referendum after three years (i.e., no earlier than February 2006).

### The State Union (2003–2006)

The State Union was from the outset a compromise widely expected to be temporary. The two republics maintained separate currencies (Montenegro used the euro; Serbia used the dinar), separate customs systems, and separate militaries until 2006. Opinion surveys throughout the union period consistently showed majority Montenegrin support for independence. In April 2005, the Montenegrin government published a timetable for a referendum, setting a target date of spring 2006.

## The 2006 Referendum

### Organisation and Threshold Dispute

The EU, through High Representative Javier Solana, brokered an agreement in November 2005 that set a 55% supermajority threshold for the referendum result to be internationally recognised—higher than the simple majority initially proposed by Montenegro. A turnout threshold of 50% was also required. The agreed question on the ballot was: *"Do you want the Republic of Montenegro to be an independent state with full international and legal subjectivity?"*

The referendum was held on **May 21, 2006**, with a turnout of 86.5%—well above the required 50%. Of valid votes cast, **55.5%** favoured independence and 44.5% opposed it (230,711 votes to 184,954 of 415,665 valid ballots), exceeding the 55% EU threshold by roughly 2,000 votes. The Organisation for Security and Co-operation in Europe (OSCE) and EU observers declared the vote free and fair. The state Referendum Commission confirmed the official result on **May 31, 2006**.

### Key Figures in the Campaign

- **Milo Đukanović**, then Prime Minister, led the pro-independence coalition. A dominant figure in Montenegrin politics since 1991, he had served alternately as president and prime minister, consistently steering Montenegro toward the EU and NATO.
- **Predrag Bulatović**, leader of the Socialist People's Party, led the "Better Together" pro-union coalition, arguing that independence would damage trade ties and isolate Montenegro economically.
- **Svetozar Marović**, the joint President of Serbia and Montenegro (the union's sole executive office), accepted the outcome and coordinated with Belgrade on the peaceful dissolution.

## Declaration and International Recognition

On **June 3, 2006**, the Assembly of the Republic of Montenegro adopted a formal Declaration of Independence. Serbia's parliament formally acknowledged the dissolution on **June 5**, and the two republics separated without incident. Serbia simultaneously declared itself the legal successor state of Serbia and Montenegro, inheriting the union's UN seat and international obligations.

Recognition came swiftly. The European Union recognised Montenegro on **June 12, 2006**, and the country was admitted to the **United Nations on June 28, 2006**, as its 192nd member. Montenegro subsequently applied for EU membership in 2008 and was granted candidate status in December 2010; accession negotiations opened in June 2012. The country joined **NATO on June 5, 2017**, becoming the alliance's 29th member.

## Significance

Montenegro's independence marked the **peaceful dissolution of the last remnant of the former Yugoslavia** and signalled the completion of the Balkans' post-Cold War political restructuring. In contrast to the bloodshed that accompanied the Yugoslav wars of the early 1990s—the Bosnian War alone killed approximately 100,000 people—the 2006 process was managed entirely through constitutional mechanisms, democratic referendum, and negotiated recognition. This outcome reflected both the EU's effective use of conditionality (the 55% threshold) as a stabilising instrument and Đukanović's tactical patience in waiting for the window set by the 2003 charter.

The dissolution also freed Serbia to begin its own EU accession process without the complication of managing a reluctant partner, while Montenegro emerged as one of the fastest-tracking Western Balkans states toward Euro-Atlantic integration. The episode became a reference point in subsequent independence discussions elsewhere in the region, including Kosovo's 2008 declaration of independence from Serbia.
