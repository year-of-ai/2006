# 2006 Thai Coup d'État

**Category**: History & Politics
**Key figures**: Thaksin Shinawatra (deposed Prime Minister); General Sonthi Boonyaratglin (Royal Thai Army commander and coup leader); King Bhumibol Adulyadej (reigning monarch); the Council for Democratic Reform under the Constitutional Monarchy (CDRM), later the Council for National Security

## Summary

On the night of **September 19, 2006**, the **Royal Thai Army** staged a bloodless coup d'état against the elected caretaker government of Prime Minister **Thaksin Shinawatra**, who was in New York City attending the United Nations General Assembly. Led by army commander **General Sonthi Boonyaratglin**, tanks and troops moved into central Bangkok and seized key government buildings and television stations without firing a shot. It was Thailand's first military coup in 15 years, ending a period that many had hoped marked the country's consolidation as a stable democracy.

The junta, styling itself the **Council for Democratic Reform under the Constitutional Monarchy (CDRM)**, moved quickly to dismantle the existing order: it abrogated the 1997 "People's Constitution," dissolved parliament and the Constitutional Court, cancelled the elections that had been scheduled for October 15, declared martial law, banned political gatherings, and imposed censorship on the media. The generals justified the takeover by citing what they described as rampant corruption, the erosion of independent state institutions, deepening social divisions, and alleged offenses against the monarchy under Thaksin's government. **King Bhumibol Adulyadej** endorsed the coup leaders shortly afterward, lending the intervention crucial legitimacy in Thai politics.

Thaksin, a telecommunications billionaire who had won landslide elections in 2001 and 2005 on the strength of populist policies popular with the rural north and northeast, did not return to contest the takeover. Speaking before leaving New York, he remarked that he had arrived as prime minister and was leaving "as an unemployed man." The junta later transformed itself into the **Council for National Security** and oversaw the drafting of a new constitution, approved by referendum in 2007, before elections were held at the end of that year.

## Significance

The 2006 coup opened a prolonged era of political instability that has shaped Thailand ever since. Rather than resolving the country's divisions, the intervention entrenched them, crystallizing a lasting confrontation between the pro-Thaksin "Red Shirt" movement — drawing on rural and working-class support — and the royalist, urban, and military-aligned "Yellow Shirt" establishment. Parties linked to Thaksin continued to win subsequent elections, only to be repeatedly removed by court rulings or force, culminating in a second coup in 2014.

For a country that many observers had regarded as one of Southeast Asia's more promising democracies, the 2006 coup was a decisive reversal that reasserted the military and palace as the ultimate arbiters of Thai politics. It demonstrated the fragility of electoral legitimacy in the face of entrenched establishment power and became a defining case study in "coups against elected populists," reverberating through Thai constitutional debates and street politics for well over a decade.
